# Ethics in AI-Assisted Research, From Principle to Behavior

The previous booklet defined the citation verification discipline as the first concrete component of an ethical layer. This booklet extends that layer. It shows that ethics in artificial intelligence assisted academic research is not a section added afterward but a workflow document built from the start. The aim is not to leave international consensus documents as abstract principles but to turn them into concrete behavioral rules in the Turkish and Greek context. This booklet is the guide's backbone, because it carries the ethical foundation of clinical and human subject research.

## 1. Why From Principle to Behavior

Most texts written on ethics remain at the level of principles. Transparency, accountability, honesty. These principles are correct, but on their own they do not tell a researcher what to do on a Tuesday morning. The gap between principle and behavior is the source of nearly all artificial intelligence ethics violations in the real world. A researcher believes in transparency but does not know how to disclose artificial intelligence assistance. They defend accountability but have not clarified who bears responsibility for a fabricated citation.

This booklet aims to close that gap. It binds each principle to a behavior. Instead of an abstract call to "be transparent," a rule of "disclose artificial intelligence use in these areas, in this form." Resnik and Hosseini (2025), arguing that the use of artificial intelligence in scientific research requires new guidance for a new tool, show that existing ethical frameworks do not yet fully meet this new situation. Mittelstadt and colleagues (2016), in mapping the ethics of algorithms, reveal how abstract principles fork in concrete context. This booklet clarifies that forking for the social scientist.

## 2. The 2024 Revision of the Declaration of Helsinki

The ethical foundation of human subject research is the Declaration of Helsinki. The World Medical Association (2025) published the most recent revision of the Declaration in JAMA, and this revision, adopted in 2024, brought the first comprehensive change since the previous major update. The revision added current paragraphs on participant rights, informed consent, and researcher responsibility. What matters for the social scientist is that this update acknowledges the new layers that digital tools and data processing add to research ethics.

The Helsinki framework is directly binding for the clinical psychologist. When a researcher conducts an artificial intelligence assisted analysis, the answers to the questions of how participant data is processed, who accesses it, and how it is protected must rest on the Helsinki principles. Informed consent must also cover the fact that the participant's data will be processed with an artificial intelligence assisted tool. This is not a clause added afterward but a design decision built from the start.

## 3. The Combination of COPE, WAME, ICMJE, and STM

In the field of publication ethics, there are several institutional consensus documents on the use of artificial intelligence, and they converge in a single coherent framework. The Committee on Publication Ethics (2023), in its position statement on authorship and artificial intelligence tools, makes it clear that artificial intelligence cannot be an author but that its use must be disclosed. The World Association of Medical Editors (2023), in its recommendations on chatbots and generative artificial intelligence, reinforces the same principle. The International Committee of Medical Journal Editors (2024), in its recommendations for the conduct and reporting of scholarly work, requires that artificial intelligence use be explained in the methods section.

These three documents combine with a fourth layer. STM, the International Association of Scientific, Technical and Medical Publishers (2025), offers a framework for the classification of artificial intelligence use in manuscript preparation. This framework divides artificial intelligence contribution into levels, from mere editing to full drafting. The disclosure standard on which this booklet rests is the combination of these four documents. The practical consequence for a researcher is the following. Artificial intelligence use is always disclosed, the detail of the disclosure varies with the level of use, but the disclosure itself can never be skipped under any condition. Jobin and colleagues (2019), measuring the global landscape of artificial intelligence ethics guidelines, showed empirically that the principles of different institutions largely overlap. This overlap legitimizes combining the four documents into a single framework.

## 4. EU AI Act Article 50 and Its Effect on Clinical Research

The European Union Artificial Intelligence Act (European Parliament and Council, 2024) establishes a regulatory framework for artificial intelligence systems. The part most directly relevant to academic research is Article 50, which governs transparency obligations. This article introduces transparency requirements for the providers and deployers of certain artificial intelligence systems. The marking of artificial intelligence generated content as having been generated is part of these obligations.

Its effect on clinical research is the following. Because Greece is a member of the European Union, the EU Artificial Intelligence Act applies directly. When a Greek researcher produces content with an artificial intelligence assisted tool, the marking of that content becomes a legal obligation. Because Turkey is not an EU member, it is not the direct addressee of the act, but the act applies in effect to the flow of data and content from Turkey to the EU. When a Turkish researcher submits to a journal in the EU, they encounter the transparency expectations of the EU framework. For this reason Article 50 holds practical importance for researchers in both countries.

## 5. KVKK and GDPR Compared, for Clinical Data

When clinical data is at stake, Turkey and Greece offer two parallel legal frameworks. In Turkey, Law No. 6698 on the Protection of Personal Data (Personal Data Protection Authority, 2016) sets clinical data apart as special category personal data. This distinction foregrounds the quality of explicit consent and the principle of data minimization for clinical data. Because Greece is a member of the European Union, the General Data Protection Regulation, that is, the GDPR, applies directly. The European Data Protection Board (2024), in its guidance on artificial intelligence and personal data, defines the compatibility of artificial intelligence assisted processing with data protection principles.

The structural similarity of the two frameworks is high, both share the principles of data minimization and purpose limitation. The practical consequence is clear for the clinical psychologist. An artificial intelligence assisted vault contains no non-anonymized clinical data. A case, a transcript, or an observation is processed only de-identified and within the framework of ethics board approval. The ethics regulation of the Turkish Psychological Association completes this legal framework with a professional layer, defining the internal ethical responsibility of the clinical psychologist. These three layers, the legal framework, professional ethics, and the institutional ethics board, together form a complete protective net for the clinical researcher.

## 6. The Consolidation of NISO Disclosure Standards

Disclosure standards have not yet converged under a single global norm, but a consolidation process is under way. The National Information Standards Organization, that is, NISO, is continuing work to develop a common framework for artificial intelligence disclosure. When this work is complete, the different disclosure forms of different publishers may converge on a single standard. This booklet records that the process was ongoing at its date of publication, and for this reason rests its disclosure standard not on a single specific document but on the combination of the current consensus. The practical advice for a researcher is to focus on the existence of disclosure rather than its form. As standards converge the form may change, but the obligation to disclose does not.

## 7. The Limits of Authorship and Disclosure

The basic rule is summarized in two sentences. Artificial intelligence cannot be an author, but its use must be disclosed. Authorship, as the Committee on Publication Ethics (2023) defines it, requires taking responsibility for the content of the work, and an artificial intelligence system cannot bear that responsibility. A model cannot defend the accuracy of the content it produces, cannot answer a reviewer's question, cannot account for an ethics violation. For this reason authorship is always with the human.

Disclosure, on the other hand, is mandatory. A text written with artificial intelligence assistance is not a text written by artificial intelligence, but the existence of that assistance cannot be hidden. The detail of disclosure varies with the level of assistance. Mere grammar correction and full draft generation require disclosure at different levels. The framework on which this booklet rests divides artificial intelligence contribution into five levels, from mere editing to full drafting. Each level is stated explicitly in the disclosure. What matters is that the disclosure be clear enough not to mislead the reader.

## 8. The Behavior Document, This Booklet Itself

This booklet applies the principle it describes to itself, and this application is the strongest behavioral example. At the head of this text there is a structured disclosure block. That block states clearly which artificial intelligence tool was used, in which role, at which contribution level, and the status of human review. The disclosure level of this booklet is recorded as co-drafting, because the author's clinical psychology expertise is directly in the content. The human review status is marked as pending until the principal author review is complete.

This is an abstract principle turned into a concrete behavior. When a researcher prepares an article with artificial intelligence assistance, they apply the same discipline. In the methods section or a disclosure note, they state the tool used, its role, and the contribution level. This disclosure does not weaken the article, it strengthens it, because the reader knows exactly what was produced and how. Transparency is not a confession of weakness but an indicator of integrity.

This reflexive demonstration is stronger than abstract instruction, because it displays the principle in the form of a behavior. In clinical psychology training, whatever the difference is between explaining how a case is formulated and showing a formulation step by step, the same difference holds here. A reader sees the disclosure block at the head of this booklet, and grasps concretely how to adapt the same block to their own article. Ethics at this point ceases to be a list of rules and becomes a model of practice. For the clinical researcher this is especially important, because clinical ethics is the domain not of abstract principles but of concrete decisions before the patient. A booklet carrying its own disclosure proves that ethics is a living practice.

## 9. An Artificial Intelligence Component for an Example IRB Application

The ethics board application is the most concrete place where principle turns into behavior. An ethics board application for an artificial intelligence assisted study must contain several additional components. First, which artificial intelligence tool will be used at which research stage. Data analysis, literature review, text editing. Second, how participant data will be processed with the artificial intelligence assisted tool and the compatibility of this processing with the data protection framework. Third, how artificial intelligence use will be explained in the informed consent text.

A concrete example is a clinical psychology study. The researcher plans to use a artificial intelligence tool in the thematic coding of interview transcripts. The application must state the following. Transcripts are de-identified before entering the vault, coding is done with artificial intelligence support but every code is verified by a human, and no non-anonymized data is transferred to the artificial intelligence tool. These three rules are written explicitly in the ethics board application. Participant protection, which stands at the center of the Helsinki framework (World Medical Association, 2025), is not a technical formality but the basis of trust. The artificial intelligence component must be added without weakening that trust.

## 10. Operational Ethics Checklist

The following twelve-item checklist turns the principles of this booklet into concrete steps a researcher can apply in every project.

1. Artificial intelligence use was defined at the start of the project as a disclosure plan.
2. Which tool will be used at which stage was documented.
3. No non-anonymized clinical data entered the vault.
4. Participant data was processed in compatibility with the data protection framework.
5. Informed consent covered the artificial intelligence assisted processing.
6. The ethics board application stated the artificial intelligence component explicitly.
7. All citations were verified with triple-index triangulation.
8. The artificial intelligence contribution level was determined on the five-level scale.
9. The disclosure appeared in the methods section or a disclosure note.
10. Authorship was attributed only to humans who bear responsibility.
11. Transparency obligations were observed for submission to the EU.
12. The final text was approved by the principal author with an integrity reading.

This list, once internalized, makes ethics a workflow rather than an obstacle.
